# Chapter 2 Friendship Presentation

## Basic framing

- Course: Communication Theory
- Text: danah boyd, "Friendship," chapter 2 in *Hanging Out, Messing Around, and Geeking Out: Kids Living and Learning with New Media* (MIT Press, 2010)
- Recommended duration: 45 minutes
- Suggested structure:
  - 30 minutes presentation
  - 8 minutes in-class activity
  - 7 minutes discussion and wrap-up

## Slide 1

### Friendship in Networked Publics

- danah boyd, chapter 2 of *Hanging Out, Messing Around, and Geeking Out*
- Communication Theory presentation
- Core question:
  - What changes when friendship becomes visible, persistent, searchable, and platform-mediated?

Speaker note:
Open with the idea that this is not "a chapter about teens using social media." It is a chapter about how communication infrastructures reshape friendship, status, self-presentation, and peer publics.

## Slide 2

### Why this chapter? Why me?

- I chose this chapter because I am interested in how everyday communication technologies shape identity, belonging, and social pressure.
- Friendship looks private, but it is actually one of the most theory-rich communication sites:
  - self-presentation
  - audience management
  - status negotiation
  - platform affordances
- My takeaway:
  - this chapter helps us see social media not as a separate world, but as part of how social life is organized

Speaker note:
If you want, personalize this slide further with one sentence from your own life, for example: "I was struck by how recognizable these dynamics still feel on Instagram, WeChat, Xiaohongshu, and TikTok."

## Slide 3

### Author, book, and research context

- Book editor and lead organizer: Mizuko Ito
- Chapter 2 lead author: danah boyd
- Project base:
  - The Digital Youth Project
  - MacArthur Foundation Digital Media and Learning initiative
- Method:
  - multi-sited collaborative ethnography
  - fieldwork roughly 2005-2008
  - interviews, observations, online/offline youth practices
- Communication value:
  - not just "effects" research
  - focuses on meaning-making, interaction, and everyday practice

Speaker note:
Highlight that the chapter is especially useful for communication theory because it studies mediated interaction as lived social practice rather than reducing media to simple causal variables.

## Slide 4

### Historical and cultural context

- U.S. teen peer culture is strongly shaped by:
  - school-centered social life
  - age segregation
  - adult monitoring
  - commercial youth culture
- In the 1980s, the mall symbolized teen hanging out
- By the mid-2000s, MySpace, Facebook, IM, and mobile phones became key infrastructures of peer sociability
- Important shift:
  - hanging out no longer ends when friends leave school

Discussion prompt:
Is social media mainly replacing offline friendship spaces, or extending them into new time-space conditions?

## Slide 5

### The chapter's main claim

- Social media do not invent teen friendship
- They intensify, extend, and reformat existing peer dynamics
- boyd's focus:
  - how technology helps establish friendships
  - how it reinforces friendship ties
  - how it complicates social boundaries
  - how it can damage relationships through visibility and drama

Strong line to say aloud:
"The issue is not whether teens are social online. The issue is what kind of sociality platforms make easier, harder, more visible, and more consequential."

## Slide 6

### Core concept: networked publics

- boyd argues that social media create "networked publics"
- These publics differ from older public spaces because they are shaped by:
  - persistence
  - searchability
  - replicability
  - scalability
- Communication-theory implication:
  - platforms are not neutral channels
  - they structure what can be seen, remembered, circulated, and judged

Speaker note:
This is the theoretical hinge of the chapter. You can define affordances here: the platform does not determine behavior, but it organizes possibilities and pressures.

## Slide 7

### Contribution 1: friendship is a communication infrastructure

- Teens mostly use social media with people they already know from school and local life
- So the online/offline split is misleading
- Friendship-driven participation is central:
  - chatting
  - checking in
  - monitoring each other
  - displaying closeness
  - staying in the loop
- Main point:
  - mediated communication sustains peer publics even when bodies are apart

Theoretical bridge:
This resembles media ecology and uses-and-gratifications, but boyd goes further by showing that friendship is socially organized, not just individually chosen.

## Slide 8

### Contribution 2: four friendship negotiations

- The chapter organizes analysis around four recurring negotiations:
  - making friends
  - performing friendships
  - articulating friendship hierarchies
  - navigating status, attention, and drama
- This is useful because it shifts us from asking "Is social media good or bad?" to asking:
  - What communicative work are platforms being used for?

Discussion prompt:
Which of these four negotiations feels most central on today's platforms?

## Slide 9

### Example 1: making friends

- Teens usually meet friends through existing peer networks, not random strangers
- New media help weak ties become stronger ties
- But friend requests and public connection lists also make inclusion and exclusion visible
- Communication issue:
  - who gets recognized?
  - who stays peripheral?

Mini-analysis:
Even a simple "add friend" action is a public speech act about affiliation.

## Slide 10

### Example 2: performing friendship

- Friendship is not only felt; it is displayed
- Comments, profile interactions, photos, and public exchanges become performances of closeness
- This connects strongly to Goffman's dramaturgical theory:
  - friendships are enacted before audiences
  - impression management becomes collective, not just individual
- But platforms complicate backstage/frontstage boundaries

Discussion prompt:
When friendship becomes visible content, does performance strengthen intimacy or hollow it out?

## Slide 11

### Example 3: ranking friends

- MySpace "Top Friends" forced social hierarchy into explicit form
- boyd shows that platforms can convert ambiguous relations into ranked visibility
- What was once tactfully vague becomes numerically legible
- Communication-theory relevance:
  - interfaces can formalize status
  - design choices become social theory in action

Critical question:
What are today's equivalents of "Top Friends"?
Possible answers: close friends lists, streaks, likes, reposts, algorithmic visibility, mutual follows.

## Slide 12

### Example 4: status, attention, and drama

- Social media amplify gossip, rumor, jealousy, and conflict
- The chapter argues this drama is usually not new, but reformatted
- Key change:
  - larger audiences
  - more persistence
  - faster spread
  - harder context interpretation
- boyd's insight:
  - platform visibility raises the stakes of ordinary peer conflict

Useful quote to paraphrase:
The drama is often the same drama as before, but the format changes the intensity.

## Slide 13

### Theoretical dialogue

- Goffman:
  - self-presentation becomes networked and audience-collapsed
- Bourdieu:
  - friendship can function like social capital, but platforms make capital visible and countable
- Affordance theory:
  - technological features shape interaction without mechanically determining it
- Habermas, as a contrast:
  - these are not rational-critical publics
  - they are affective, status-laden, youth peer publics

My argument:
boyd is strongest when she treats friendship as an infrastructural communication problem rather than a moral panic issue.

## Slide 14

### My critique and update

- Strengths:
  - rich ethnographic detail
  - avoids technological determinism
  - takes teens seriously as social actors
- Limits:
  - U.S.-centered and school-centered
  - based on the MySpace/Facebook era
  - underplays platform capitalism and algorithmic curation
  - less attention to race/class/gender differences than today's scholarship might demand
- Update for 2026:
  - friendship performance is now even more entangled with recommendation systems, influencers, screenshots, and cross-platform circulation

Discussion prompt:
Does algorithmic recommendation now shape friendship as much as peer choice does?

## Slide 15

### Activity: redesign a friendship platform

- Format:
  - 2 minutes individual thinking
  - 3 minutes pair discussion
  - 3 minutes share-out
- Task:
  - Imagine you are designing a social platform for friendship rather than attention extraction
  - choose one feature to remove, redesign, or add
- Possible targets:
  - likes
  - friend counts
  - read receipts
  - ranking systems
  - screenshots
  - algorithmic feeds
- Required theory question:
  - Which communication problem does your redesign solve?
  - Which new problem might it create?

Why this works:
Students must apply boyd's framework and confront the politics of platform design.

## Slide 16

### Meme option if you want a more playful activity

Speaker note:
This version is more fun and often gets stronger participation, but keep the debrief theoretical.

## Slide 17

### Conclusion

- Friendship is not outside media systems
- Platforms do not replace social life; they reorganize its visibility, temporality, and stakes
- The chapter's lasting value:
  - it gives us a vocabulary for thinking about friendship as mediated social practice
- Final takeaway question:
  - If friendship is always communicatively performed, what kinds of friendship do platforms make imaginable?
